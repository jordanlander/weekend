# How a Fast, Affordable Website Helped Local Artist Rebecca Coppock Bring Kindness to Life

![heroImage](https://rebeccacoppock.com/assets/artist-with-dolls-captioned-Cf7hPE4f.webp)

Sometimes the best businesses start with a simple mission: spread a little more kindness in the world.

That's exactly what happened when Rebecca Coppock, a former art teacher from Erie, Pennsylvania, decided to turn her passion for creating into something bigger. Today, her handmade upcycled dolls bring joy to families across the country: and it all started with one weekend and a simple website.

## From Classroom to Creative Studio

Rebecca spent years teaching art, watching her students discover their creative voices. But after her son's involvement with the Erie Pride Festival opened her eyes to the power of community and acceptance, she felt called to create something that could spread that same message of love and kindness.

Enter the dolls.

Not your typical stuffed animals, Rebecca's creations are what she lovingly calls "hand-stitched souls made from stories and sweaters." Each doll is crafted from recycled clothing: old sweaters, vintage shirts, forgotten fabrics that get a second chance at life. Every single one has a name, a personality, and a little story that comes with it.

![image_1](https://rebeccacoppock.com/assets/ellie-B5iSJ8DS.jpg)

Take Ellie, described as "soft and cuddly," or Charlie, who's "bold and artsy." There's Willow, the "guardian of tender moments," and dozens of other unique characters waiting to be adopted. Rebecca doesn't just make dolls: she creates companions with soul.

Her brand name says it all: "Little Panda Acts of Kindness." Every doll represents a small act of love, a reminder that kindness can be stitched into the fabric of our daily lives.

## The Digital Dilemma Every Artist Faces

Rebecca had the talent, the passion, and the product. What she didn't have was a way to reach people beyond her local community.

Like many artists, she faced the same frustrating choice: spend thousands on a fancy website that might take months to build, or stick to social media and hope people find her.

Social media was great for sharing her work: her Instagram and Facebook pages under "Little Panda Acts of Kindness" were growing steadily. But when potential customers wanted to learn more, see her full collection, or place custom orders, they needed somewhere to go.

That's where things got complicated. Traditional web design agencies quoted her prices in the thousands and timelines stretching weeks or months. For someone just starting to grow her creative business, those numbers felt impossible.
